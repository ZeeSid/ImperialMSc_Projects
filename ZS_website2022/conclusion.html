Discussion

Discussion

Understanding the underlying causes of chronic inflammation could inform on the aetiology of complex diseases. This project aimed to identify genetic loci that have common effects on biochemical markers associated with low-grade inflammation, that may contribute to or alleviate these multifactorial conditions. These traits were IL-6interleukin-6, insulin, fibrinogen, LDLlow-density lipoprotein, monocytes, triglycerides and vitamin D.

LDSC was used to evaluate which of the input traits are genetically correlated on a genome-wide level. In the pairwise comparison of all the traits, only LDLlow-density lipoprotein and triglyceride levels showed a statistically significant genetic correlation, although this does not confirm with certainty that none of the other phenotypes share pleiotropic loci. In a subset meta-analysis, after clumping to identify independent significant SNPsSingle Nucleotide Polymorphism, lead SNPsSingle Nucleotide Polymorphism from 71 genomic risk loci were found. Meta-analysis confirmed that 16/71 SNPsSingle Nucleotide Polymorphism were associated with more than one trait, and nine out of these 16 had effects on at least one trait not previously associated with that particular SNPSingle Nucleotide Polymorphism.

Functional annotation using FUMA highlighted links between five of these 16 pleiotropic SNPsSingle Nucleotide Polymorphism and the disease retinitis pigmentosa. The traits associated with the non-coding intergenic regions RP11, and the genes DHX38 and POC1B were LDLlow-density lipoprotein, triglycerides, IL-6interleukin-6 and monocytes. This provides new evidence that RPretinitis pigmentosa is an inflammatory condition, as there is contradictory research about this disease classification.


Conclusions

The limitations of cross-phenotype analysis should be considered when reviewing any results. Although meta-analysis is powerful, the sizes of the cohorts from each individual study can mean that smaller studies are underpowered. For example, fibrinogen, IL-6interleukin-6 and insulin each had around 10,000 participants in their original GWASGenome-wide association study, whereas the other four traits had around 100,000 (Table 1, Methods). This is reflected in the results: IL-6interleukin-6 and fibrinogen had to be excluded from parts of this research, and both insulin and IL-6interleukin-6 were rarely associated with any of the lead SNPsSingle Nucleotide Polymorphism in the meta-analysis. Another feature of the data selected is that all participants are European in order to avoid inaccurate results due to confounding by population stratification. This means that the results are not directly applicable to individuals of other ethnicities. Additionally, rare variants could not be detected due to the MAF cut-off of 0.01 in the quality control process of LDSC.

With regards to the pleiotropic SNPsSingle Nucleotide Polymorphism identified, the interpretation of this work has focused on implications for RPretinitis pigmentosa. However, it is possible that the variants are unrelated to the disease aetiology. More evidence is needed to substantiate the hypothesis linking chronic inflammation to RPretinitis pigmentosa.


Future work

To build upon the findings of this work, a GWASGenome-wide association study or family linkage analysis focused on RPretinitis pigmentosa could aid understanding of whether this disease is related to inflammation, and experiments with cell culture could examine whether anti-inflammatory treatments slow the progression of retinal degeneration.

It would also be ideal to investigate a larger number of traits that are related to the chronic inflammatory state, either using summary statistics when they become available or with individual-level data from a data repository such as the UK BioBank [33]. If further associations are uncovered it could help to provide a biological explanation for chronic inflammation, particularly when coupled with more extensive functional annotation such as expression quantitative trait loci and pathway analyses. All of this could add weight to the hypothesis that these SNPsSingle Nucleotide Polymorphism are biologically pleiotropic, as opposed to being spuriously associated. In turn, this could suggest potential causes of chronic inflammation which are largely unknown, and provide necessary insight into complex diseases.
